What Makes a Good Aerothermal PhD?

Alexander Reaves
Last updated on Jul 2, 2022 5 min read
Image credit: Unsplash

What makes a good aerothermal PhD? Or, I guess more generally just what makes any PhD a good one ?

I recently had the opportunity to listen a presentation by Dr. Tim Williams, Dr. Ben Mohankumar, and soon-to-be Dr. Yuri Frey on their thoughts on these matters. I thought that the talk was very helpful for thinking about how I should approach my PhD. Hopefully anyone reading this blog post will be able to benefit from their advice as well.

1. Day-to-Day Advice

  • Ask an important question of your PhD. If you ask a significant question, even if you fail in getting an answer, your work will still be highly regarded.
  • Never assume anything.
  • You must justify and own the approach that you choose. Weigh up advice and other’s experience, and consider the correct way to move forward. Repeating the existing method is not always the most efficient route.
  • Prioritise speed over realism for CFD or experiment activities, particularly early in the project. Move quickly, and learn a lot. Repeating tests for your final data rarely takes more than a few weeks.
  • Do not be cautious when spending money for purchases that will improve the speed and quality of your research. Discuss with your supervisor, and order promptly.
  • Material costs are not as significant as the cost of technician time – factor this in when considering where to source items.
  • Student-technician relationship on an experimental project is a team – regular communication vital to making everyone’s job easier.
  • Reliability and repeatability of experimental measurements are improved by adopting a thorough routine. This helps to develop a feel for your rig, and identify which conditions affect the data acquired, and when maintenance may be needed. An example given to improve consistency was to match the tasks to start up the rig to a particular piece of music, so consider how a similar ritual could work for you.

2. Working with academic and industrial supervisors

  • Aligning objectives of research (with industry/project milestones) – set out goals early and clearly. This should be the primary objective for your project proposal dissertation. Think long term, and factor in that the scope and duration of planned tasks will change.
  • Regular supervisor input is valuable – arranging regular meetings is good to set expectations, but developing an (considerate) open door policy for short questions will improve the use of your time.
  • See industrial contacts as a gateway to the company’s experience rather than an assessor of your output. They will be excited to participate and learn from your research too.
  • Scheduling to share your work and to discuss relevant developments from your industrial partner around every 4 weeks benefits all parties.
  • Ask for help!

3. Managing setbacks / uncertainty

  • Expect frequent failures. Only a small fraction of your work will be included in your thesis.
  • The best way to do something is to just do it. The sooner you make mistakes, learn how to anticipate, and respond to them, the faster you will get to your final dataset. Everyone makes mistakes, and you will learn from it.
  • If unsure where to begin, try recreating or analysing pre-existing work. It will give trust to your work if you show that you have been able to recreate something before moving on to something novel. Ask colleagues at the lab for data or for CFD that you could reuse to give you grounding or training.
  • Do not worry about how your progress compares to others. It is the worst available use of your time.
  • Ask for help (continued)! You will have a lot to tackle in your project, and will frequently come up against uncertainty. Your colleagues and supervisors are often happy to help (or can point you in the right direction if unable to do so) so do not feel bad about asking for advice.

4. Publications, conferences, and patents

  • Be aware of intellectual property concerns – papers containing proprietary information must be submitted to industrial partners for clearance weeks before conference/journal submission. Disagreements on publishable content are very rare, and required edits will usually consist of non-dimensionalising data and removing revealing values from figure axes.
  • Patents can help you publish more by removing confidentiality constraints, and are also tangible outputs from your work.
  • Writing a paper demands that you refine understanding from a part of your research, and will help write up that section more quickly. Conference exposure can reveal where relevant work is taking place.
  • Practise telling your story as much as you can within the lab/department. You will get good feedback and good questions to prepare you for sharing the work externally.

5. Record keeping and writing up

  • Keeping a research diary consolidates current understanding, and provides a useful reference later. For experiments, log ambient conditions, highlights of the data as the experiment runs, sketches of the probe orientation and experiment set up, note what other rig is running at the same time. (Buy fancy pens and pleasing paper, whatever encourages you to commit to writing the work you do with your hands!)
  • Keep a consistent record of test data (experimental and computational). This will save a lot of time and worry when you come to review it, sometimes years later.
  • Save and sensibly label scripts that you have written to plot out and present data.
  • Write down explanations of your findings regularly. You do not fully understand something until you can explain it clearly in writing.
  • Write up once you have the data available to satisfy your research objectives. Question adding supplementary work that is not key to your thesis – opportunities for further investigation will develop from your work, but can form part of future projects.

6. Outside your research / taking time out

  • Keeping a routine leads to a better division of your time and efforts. Rate of progress will vary wildly during your PhD, but is achieved best through consistent input. Taking a longer break from and returning to challenging tasks often reveals solutions with renewed perspective, and the chance to ask others.
  • Teatime (or a non-Whittle Lab equivalent) can be a good break from work, a chance to ask the general lab consensus a question on which you could use other’s experience, and keeps you up to date on goings-on.
  • Say yes to teaching or supervising opportunities. Undertaking teaching places individual responsibility on you, and the skills developed translate to teamwork and management.
